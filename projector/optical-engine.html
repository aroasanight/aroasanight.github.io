Optical Engine

This is strongly based off an extract from howstuffworks.com - you'll find a link to the source page at the bottom of this page. I've added some more bits for clarity myself, such as the parts about the light integrator rod, LCDs, and the lens, but for the most part it's the same.

To understand how an LCD projector works, it's best to start at the beginning -- with a beam of light -- and end on the movie screen itself.

Step one: A powerful light source emits a beam of intense, white light. This then passes through a light integrator rod, homogenizing rod, or light tunnel (all names for the same thing) - which distributes the light from the bulb evenly over the rectangle, which will eventually become the image projected onto the screen. Without the light integrater rod, you'd end up with a vignette effect on the screen - since the bulb would likely emit more light from its center instead of the corners.

Step two: Our beam of white light bounces off a group of dichroic mirrors - which are mirrors coated in a special film that reflects only a specified wavelength of light - in this case red, green, and blue. You know how a prism (or a droplet of water) breaks a beam of light into distinct wavelengths (or a rainbow of colors)? The same principle applies here, only each dichroic mirror breaks off a single specified wavelength. So the white light hits the mirrors, and each reflects a beam of colored light on through the projector: one red, one green and one blue.

Step three: The beams of red, green, and blue light each pass through their own liquid crystal display composed of thousands of tiny pixels. Each pixel contains liquid crystals that can manipulate light when an electric current is applied. These liquid crystals can change their alignment, allowing them to either block light or let it pass through. In a typical LCD, two polarizing filters sit on either side of the liquid crystal layer (polarization is the process by which light waves vibrate in a particular direction). When light hits the first filter, it becomes polarized. As the liquid crystals adjust their alignment in response to the electric current, they control how much light passes through to the second filter, creating different shades of gray. The three LCD screens display the red, green, and blue components of the image separately - for example, to transmit white, all three will allow all light through, but for purple, only the red and blue will allow light through.

Step four: But of course the final image we see isn't red, green or blue; it's full color. So inside the LCD projector, the three tinted versions of this scene recombine in a dichroic prism (a finely crafted combination of four triangular prisms) to form a single image composed of not three colors, but millions of colors.

Step five: The light of this vibrant, colorful version of the scene then passes through a projector lens and onto the big screen. The lens allows for focusing the image, and on some projectors also increasing/decreasing zoom. This is so you could use it as a short-throw projector, with the projector close to the screen, or have it further away just by adjusting a dial.
